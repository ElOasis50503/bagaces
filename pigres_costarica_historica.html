at the mouth of the Río Tárcoles, Gulf of Nicoya

A single plate documenting the Río Tárcoles where it meets tidal water at Pigres, on the Gulf of Nicoya — the Pacific counterpart to the Atlantic river scenes catalogued elsewhere in this archive.

P-01
Postcard · H. N. Rudd, Costa Rica · Pacific Slope

Río Pigres

A lone boatman holds a small skiff against the current, his pole planted in the shallows beside the bleached, root-tangled skeleton of a fallen tree. The water lies almost flat under an overcast sky, the far bank a solid wall of riparian forest closing the view downstream — the unmistakable hush of a tidal river at low water, well away from any town.

The card is captioned simply "Pigres" — a settlement on the Gulf of Nicoya named for the spot where the Río Tárcoles, swollen by the Virilla and the Río Grande de San Ramón upstream, finally reaches salt water. It is the Pacific-side counterpart to the Atlantic river scenes elsewhere in this archive: the same patient labor of pole against current, the same forest pressing close on both banks, but here ending in the Gulf of Nicoya rather than the Caribbean.
