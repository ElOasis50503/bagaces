The Combatants of Santa Rosa

Costa Rican volunteers, hours before the battle
January 15, 1955 · Casona de Santa Rosa, Guanacaste

In the hours before the fighting, a photographer gathered a group of Costa Rican volunteers on the stone wall and corredor of the historic Casona de Santa Rosa. They hold bolt-action rifles, wear a mix of uniform and civilian dress, and pose for the camera — some smiling, others grave. Three hours after the shutter clicked, several of the men in this photograph were dead. These two images are, as a result, among the most affecting documents in the archive: a group portrait taken on the very threshold of the most intense battle of the 1955 invasion.

The Casona as the stage for two wars

The Hacienda Santa Rosa, in La Cruz, Guanacaste, was already a site charged with historical meaning long before 1955. It was here, on March 20, 1856, that Costa Rican troops under General José Joaquín Mora drove out William Walker's filibusters in barely fourteen minutes, in one of the founding episodes of Costa Rican national identity. Nearly a century later, the same corredor and the same stone corrals filled once more with armed men — this time government volunteers under President José Figueres Ferrer, facing an invasion organized by exiled calderonistas with the backing of Nicaraguan dictator Anastasio Somoza.

For Costa Ricans of the period, the coincidence of place was no rhetorical accident: it stood as living proof that, a century on, Santa Rosa remained the point where the nation defended itself against foreign invasion.

The battle of January 15

The 1955 invasion had begun days earlier, on January 7, with calderonista forces crossing over from Nicaraguan territory. After clashes in Villa Quesada and near the Río Peje, the government advance — under the command of Domingo García — pushed the invading forces northward through Guanacaste, until the largest confrontation of the entire campaign converged, almost symbolically, on the very ground of Santa Rosa.

Journalist Joaquín Vargas Gené, present in the area during the fighting, called it possibly the most intense battle ever recorded in the country's history. Official Estado Mayor bulletins, published over the following days, gave casualty figures that varied considerably from one communiqué to the next — a pattern typical of wartime reporting at the time, which tended to magnify enemy losses while minimizing one's own.

Context of the conflict

An invasion organized from Nicaragua by exiled calderonistas with the support of Anastasio Somoza, against the government of José Figueres Ferrer. Begun on January 7, 1955; the most intense fighting was concentrated in Guanacaste, particularly in La Cruz and at the Hacienda Santa Rosa.

The volunteers

Press reports of the period described each of the roughly 45 men who fought in the first battle of Santa Rosa as a national hero — language that reflects the tone of the Costa Rican press during the crisis more than a verified account of the action.

A disputed count

Sources from January 1955 do not agree with one another. A first Estado Mayor communiqué claimed the invading commander had been killed along with 34 of his men; government losses were reported at just 4 dead and a much larger number wounded. A later bulletin, two days afterward, gave different figures — at least 15 confirmed dead among the invaders and a considerable volume of captured war matériel. This discrepancy is not a transcription error: it reflects how the war was reported in real time, with figures adjusted to the political needs of the moment and no independent verification on the ground.

Reading the photograph

The two exposures — likely consecutive shots of the same framing — document something uncommon in Costa Rican war photography: an almost informal group portrait, taken minutes before the men in it entered actual combat. The mix of dress is telling. Some men wear civilian shirts and street trousers; others wear field uniform with bandoliers crossed over the chest and bayonets fixed. This unevenness confirms what contemporary accounts already noted: a substantial share of those who defended the northern border in January 1955 were not professional soldiers — Costa Rica's army had been abolished barely six years earlier — but civilian volunteers hastily armed to repel the invasion.

The raised circular seal visible in the upper right corner of both prints is consistent with an official archival or institutional ownership stamp, likely applied when the prints entered the documentary collection. A macro inspection of that seal could reveal the exact issuing body behind the physical print.
