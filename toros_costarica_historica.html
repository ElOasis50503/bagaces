Toros

Corridas a la Tica — The Improvised Bullfight

Two postcard views of Costa Rica's distinctive vernacular bullfighting tradition — not the formal corrida of Spain or Mexico, but a civic and carnival spectacle in which the crowd itself enters the ring. One plate documents the practice on the Atlantic coast at Limón; the other, a San José publisher's composite, shows the Central Valley form in close detail.

TR-01
"Bull fight in Limón" — postcard, English-caption edition, n.d.

Bull Fight in Limón

Corrida de toros en Limón

A wide field — likely the town's central recreation ground — has been turned into an improvised ring by the simple expedient of a standing crowd. There is no built barrera; the spectators themselves form the boundary, with a packed grandstand at right (its striped awning the only formal structure in sight) doing the work a proper plaza de toros would otherwise perform. A mounted rider and a loose bull occupy the cleared center.

The two-story wooden buildings at left, fronted by verandas and packed with onlookers leaning over the rail, are characteristic of Limón's turn-of-the-century streetscape — built for the tropical climate and, on a day like this, doing double duty as private box seats. The forested hill behind suggests this fiesta sits at the edge of town rather than in its commercial core.

Curator's note — Limón's fiestas cívicas have long followed a distinct rhythm from the Central Valley's, shaped by the port's Afro-Caribbean and immigrant labor communities built up around the Atlantic Railway and the United Fruit Company. This plate's informality — no fixed arena, a borrowed field, a crowd-as-fence — reads as a port-town adaptation of a tradition more architecturally elaborated in San José (compare TR-02).
TR-02
"Memorias de Costa Rica" — Toros, No. 4, Juan Knöhr Hijos, San José, C.R.

Memorias de Costa Rica — Toros

Serie "Memorias de Costa Rica," No. 4

A two-panel composite from the San José publisher Juan Knöhr Hijos, captioned simply "Toros" in both insets. The upper panel shows the field-level view: a loose company of improvisados — amateur participants, never professional matadors — fanning out on foot before a charging bull, with the packed grandstand and a turreted pavilion visible beyond the rail. The lower panel turns the camera on the crowd itself, a tiered wooden grandstand jammed with spectators, several climbing the rails for a better vantage.

This is the Central Valley's corrida a la tica in its classic form: no professional toreros, no kill, and a deliberately permeable boundary between participant and spectator that any local festival-goer could cross simply by stepping over the fence. The "Memorias de Costa Rica" series was one of several decorative postcard lines marketed explicitly as souvenirs of national life, alongside scenic and ethnographic subjects.

Curator's note — Juan Knöhr Hijos was among San José's principal postcard publishers in this period, producing decorative series alongside firms such as Lehmann and Céspedes already represented elsewhere in this archive. The grandstand architecture here — tiered, roofed, flag-trimmed — is considerably more developed than Limón's borrowed field in TR-01, consistent with San José's role as the seat of the more formally organized fiestas of the period.
