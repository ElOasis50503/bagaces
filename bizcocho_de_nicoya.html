El Bizcocho de Nicoya

Una escena ribereña de la preparación tradicional · A Riverside Scene of Traditional Preparation

Two plates from a single documentary series, Fondo Fidel Tristán, Archivo Nacional — Nicoya, Guanacaste

Bizcocho de Nicoya is a maize confection particular to the Nicoya peninsula, its preparation a household and riverside craft passed through generations of Chorotega and Zamba families in Guanacaste. The two photographs gathered here belong to a single ethnographic series documenting the process step by step: gathering the carrying-vessels at the stream, then treading the cooked maize underfoot to begin its transformation into dough.

The original archival entry describes the scene plainly: a boy and two indigenous girls of the region at a small stream, one identified as Chorotega and the other as Zamba, with an iron pot, gourd vessels (guacales), and clay basins (lebrillos) arranged at the water's edge, distributing the cooked maize among the vessels before treading it with washed feet.

PLATE I

CR-AN-AH-FO-079243 · Fondo Fidel Tristán, Documento 167 · Archivo Nacional de Costa Rica

Carrying the Vessels

Las Muchachas y los Guacales

Three girls stand ankle-deep in the slow current of a Nicoya stream, the overhanging gallery forest doubled in the still water behind them. Two balance large gourd vessels — guacales, hollowed and dried calabash — directly atop their heads in the traditional carrying posture; a third cradles hers against her hip. The gourds are likely empty here, gathered for the maize that will shortly be cooked and distributed among them at the water's edge.

The mirror-still surface and dense leaf litter along the bank suggest a quiet backwater rather than a flowing ford — the kind of sheltered stream margin favored for this work because the water stayed cool and the footing was soft underfoot.

PLATE II

CR-AN-AH-FO-079306 · Fondo Fidel Tristán, Documento 167 · Archivo Nacional de Costa Rica

Treading the Maize

El Majado con los Pies Lavados

Two girls stand barefoot in shallow wooden tubs at the stream's edge, skirts gathered and tucked against the work, in the act the archival caption describes precisely: distributing the cooked maize among the vessels and treading it underfoot, feet washed clean in the stream beforehand. An iron pot, gourd vessels, and clay lebrillos are set out within reach along the bank.

The original entry records a third figure, a boy, present in the wider scene; here the frame holds on the two girls performing the treading itself — the maize-softening step that begins the dough's transformation into bizcocho.

The catalogue identifies the girl at left as Chorotega and the girl at right as Zamba — a detail addressed below.

A Note on Chorotega and Zamba

The archive's own caption distinguishes the two girls in Plate II by heritage — one Chorotega, one Zamba — a distinction worth preserving rather than smoothing over. Chorotega refers to the Mesoamerican-affiliated peoples who gave Greater Nicoya much of its pre-Columbian material culture, from carved metates to the polychrome pottery traditions documented elsewhere in this archive. Zamba, in the historical Spanish colonial casta vocabulary of the isthmus, denoted mixed African and indigenous ancestry.

That the photographer or cataloguer recorded this distinction at all reflects a colonial-era habit of racial classification rather than necessarily anything visible in the work itself; both girls are shown performing the identical task, side by side, in the same stream. The note is preserved here as historical record, not as an organizing principle for reading the image.

On the Series

Bizcocho de Nicoya endures today as one of Guanacaste's signature maize confections, but these two plates catch it at its most laborious stage, before sugar, before the wood-fired clay oven, before the loaf takes shape — just cooked grain, a stream, and bare feet doing the first patient work of breaking it down. The vessels in Plate I and the tubs in Plate II are the same household kit, photographed moments apart in what was almost certainly a single working morning by the water.
